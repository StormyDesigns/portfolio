Overview

Keeping borrowers engaged in a competitive market

Tala is an Android application serving emerging markets — Philippines, Kenya, Mexico, and India — offering immediate credit access, quick loans, and financial accounts. Users apply for loans and gain access to checking accounts and bill pay upon approval.

I was the sole designer on Tala Rewards, an in-app loyalty program designed to incentivize borrowers to engage in positive financial behaviors. I worked alongside a product manager, researcher, product marketing manager, engineers, graphic designers, and a systems designer.

The Problem

Tala was losing users to competitors

Competing apps were pulling users away by offering:

  • Lower transfer fees and higher loan utility
  • Rewards, loyalty, and referral programs
  • Reduced friction for accessing funds

Internal data revealed a clear insight: users who engaged with multiple Tala features showed significantly higher repayment rates and longer retention. The opportunity was to make multi-feature engagement feel rewarding — not just functional.

Key insight: Multi-feature engagement correlates with higher repayment rates and retention. A rewards program could incentivize the behaviors that already predicted success.

Success Metrics

What we were aiming for

Before kicking off design, the team aligned on measurable goals to evaluate the program's impact:

5–10%
increase in early customer retention
5–10%
decrease in loan default rates
5–10%
increase in new account deposits within 30 days

Proof of concept

Before the full rewards program, a partnership between the designer, product manager, and researcher tested incentivizing account deposits through rewards. The result: a 30% increase in account deposits — validating the approach and giving the team confidence to invest in a full program.

Challenges

Designing with constraints

Three key challenges shaped how I approached this project:

Unfamiliar market

  • Feature launched in Philippines first
  • Relied on a local researcher for competitive analysis
  • User interviews were conducted in Tagalog

Developer constraints

  • Engineering team was at capacity
  • Required a simplified feature design
  • Scope had to stay lean without sacrificing quality

Missing design system

  • Worked with inconsistent previous designs while the system was being finalized
  • Collaborated closely with brand and systems designer to contribute components as we went
Research

Competitive analysis

I examined loyalty program apps in both the Philippines and the US to identify common patterns and user expectations. The analysis surfaced four key themes:

  • Prominent display of points or status at the top of the screen
  • A mix of quick-redemption and long-term rewards to maintain engagement
  • Variety in reward types to prevent user boredom over time
  • Clear guides explaining how to earn tokens — education built into the flow
Design Process

Explorations and iterations

I started by creating a navigation flow that was reviewed and approved by the product manager, engineers, and design team before moving into screens.

My first two iterations of the Rewards home page explored different approaches to surfacing key information:

  • Iteration 1: Token amount and progress bar at top, "View rewards" button, activity log for tasks, "How to earn" education section, and a list of earnable activities
  • Iteration 2: Nested rewards and activities behind earn/redeem buttons to reduce visual overwhelm

After reviewing both with the team, I identified several problems:

  • Unclear information hierarchy across the home screens
  • Nested rewards obscured the incentive — users couldn't easily see what they were working toward
  • The product manager wanted an unlimited token ceiling, not the fixed 400-token cap in the initial designs
  • Reward cards consumed excessive space on the options screen
  • Redemption confirmation lacked any celebratory moment
Refined Design

Designs for user testing

I refined the designs to address the feedback and set up for usability testing. Key updates included:

  • A bright, sticky token display with illustrations at the top of the Rewards home screen
  • A featured rewards carousel with a "View all" option — keeping rewards visible without overwhelming
  • An activities section clearly showing token amounts per action
  • An info icon linking to the "how to earn" reference
  • A bottom sheet for reward details with a conditional CTA — enabled when tokens were sufficient, disabled when they weren't

I also tested two entry point placements for the Rewards feature:

Option A

  • Bottom navigation bar

Option B

  • Top of screen, across from the Tala logo
User Testing

Moderated usability tests with 14 users

I conducted moderated usability tests with 14 users segmented by borrowing history to test two hypotheses:

Hypothesis 1

  • Users understand how to earn tokens
  • Users understand how to redeem rewards
  • Users can identify the relationship between tokens and rewards

Hypothesis 2

  • Users feel motivated to complete desired behaviors for rewards
  • Rewards feel appealing and worth the effort

Users were split into two segments:

Mature borrowers (n=7)

  • 10+ loans disbursed
  • Mixed transaction history

Casual borrowers (n=7)

  • 1–3 loans disbursed
  • Mixed transaction history

Both hypotheses validated ✓

Users clearly understood the activity-to-token mechanics and could identify redeemable vs. unredeemable rewards. They also understood that tokens would be deducted upon redemption. Areas needing clarification: minimum amounts, variable earnings, redemption frequency, and reward reusability.

On motivation: non-transacting users said they were likely to try new features for rewards. Transacting users said they'd increase their engagement. Tala-specific rewards and local options — Jollibee, supermarkets, drugstores — drove the strongest motivation.

Post-Testing Updates

Aligning with the design system

After testing, I collaborated with the brand design team and systems designer to align the final designs with Tala's newly finalized design system. One meaningful change came out of this phase: the terminology shifted from "Tokens" to "Points" — users in testing were more familiar with points from other apps, and the change improved immediate comprehension.

Reflection

Key learnings

Navigating ambiguity in an unfamiliar market. I couldn't rely on personal knowledge of the Philippines market, so I leaned heavily on the local researcher — and that partnership was essential. It reinforced how much good research partnerships matter when you're designing for a context you haven't lived in.

Contributing to a design system mid-project. Working without a finalized system was a constraint, but it also created an opportunity. By collaborating closely with the brand and systems teams throughout, I was able to contribute components and patterns that outlasted the project itself.
